Where

Plate boundaries are the easy story — the Pacific Ring of Fire, the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, the Himalayan front. Watch what happens when you drop the magnitude floor: thousands of micro-quakes appear that the news never covers, but which seismologists use to track stress build-up along faults.

Each dot is one event. Color is magnitude (cool = small, warm = large); size scales with magnitude squared so a 6.0 stands out from a 4.0.

How big, how often

The Gutenberg–Richter law says the count of earthquakes drops roughly tenfold for every unit of magnitude. A clean log-linear histogram is one of the cleanest "natural laws" in seismology — you can read the b-value straight off the slope. Drag the magnitude floor up and down: the slope holds remarkably well.

Log-scale counts per magnitude bin. The dashed line is a least-squares fit to b-value computed in real time by the runtime.

Time of day

Tectonic earthquakes don't care what time it is — but human-induced ones (mining, fluid injection, reservoir loading) often do. A dataset that's flat across the 24-hour clock is a healthy seismically-natural one. A dataset that spikes during work hours is a tell.
